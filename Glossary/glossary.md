# **Brutalism: Glossary of Key Terms**

A reference guide to core terminology used in the study of brutalism across architecture, design, and culture.

<img src="https://i.pinimg.com/736x/cf/2e/69/cf2e6945e1a33a8dea001043fc14a8c4.jpg"/>

---

## A

**Béton Brut** *(French: "raw concrete")*
The material technique that gave brutalism its name. Concrete is poured, then the formwork is removed — leaving the rough, textured surface exposed without any further finishing. Popularized by Le Corbusier, it became the defining material of brutalist architecture.

**Board-formed Concrete**
A type of béton brut where wooden planks are used as formwork, leaving the grain pattern of the wood imprinted in the finished concrete surface.

---

## C

**Constructivism**
Soviet avant-garde movement (1920s–1930s) that influenced brutalism. Constructivists similarly rejected decoration and embraced industrial materials. Key architects: El Lissitzky, Moisei Ginzburg, Alexander Vesnin.

**Corbusian**
Relating to or influenced by Le Corbusier (Charles-Édouard Jeanneret). His "Five Points of Architecture" — pilotis, roof gardens, free plan, horizontal windows, free façade — became foundational to brutalism.

---

## E

**Exposed Structure**
A defining brutalist feature: structural elements (beams, columns, slabs, ducts, pipes) are left visible rather than hidden behind cladding or plaster. The building shows how it is made.

---

## F

**Five Points of Architecture**
Le Corbusier's foundational principles (1927):
1. Pilotis (columns elevating the building)
2. Free façade (non-structural exterior)
3. Free plan (interior walls not load-bearing)
4. Horizontal windows (*fenêtres en longueur*)
5. Roof garden

**Formwork**
The temporary mold into which concrete is poured. The texture of the formwork material (wood, metal, plastic) is transferred to the concrete surface, becoming part of the finished design in béton brut.

**Functionalism**
The principle that the form of a building (or object) should be determined by its function. A core tenet of brutalism: nothing is added that does not serve a purpose.

---

## G

**Glitch Aesthetic**
In digital design, the deliberate simulation or inclusion of digital errors — compression artifacts, pixelation, signal noise — as a design choice. The digital equivalent of exposed imperfection in brutalist architecture.

---

## I

**In-Situ Concrete**
Concrete poured on-site (as opposed to precast). In-situ concrete produces unique textures and imperfections at each location, making it architecturally distinct.

---

## M

**Megastructure**
A very large architectural structure intended to house a significant portion of a city's functions. Brutalist megastructures (e.g., Barbican Estate, London; Habitat 67, Montreal) attempted to create self-contained urban environments.

**Modernism**
The broad architectural and cultural movement (late 19th century onwards) that rejected historical styles and ornament in favor of new materials, technologies, and forms. Brutalism is a sub-movement within modernism.

**Monumentalism**
The use of massive scale, imposing forms, and grand gestures to create a sense of permanence, power, or significance. Common in both Soviet and Western brutalism.

---

## N

**New Brutalism**
The specific term coined by British architects Alison and Peter Smithson in the 1950s. Distinguished from mere use of raw concrete — New Brutalism was an ethical as well as aesthetic position, emphasizing honesty, community, and the rejection of superficial styling.

---

## P

**Panel Construction** *(Панельное строительство)*
Soviet prefabricated concrete panel building technique, used to rapidly construct mass housing (Khrushchevkas, Brezhnevkas). Related to but distinct from brutalism — panel buildings prioritized speed and economy over sculptural form.

**Pilotis**
Load-bearing columns (from Le Corbusier's vocabulary) that elevate a building off the ground, creating an open ground floor. A signature brutalist feature.

**Precast Concrete**
Concrete elements cast in a factory, then transported and assembled on-site. Allows precise repetition of modular forms. Used extensively in brutalist housing to create consistent, large-scale facades.

---

## R

**Raw Aesthetics**
The visual quality of unfinished or unrefined surfaces — exposed concrete, visible welds, rough edges. A positive value in brutalist design: rawness signals honesty.

---

## S

**Social Housing**
Publicly funded residential buildings. Brutalism was closely tied to social housing programs in Britain (estates), France (*grands ensembles*), and the Soviet Union (microrayons), sharing the utopian belief that good architecture could improve social conditions.

**Street Deck** *(Streets in the Sky)*
An influential concept by Alison and Peter Smithson: elevated walkways connecting housing blocks, intended to replicate the social functions of a street at height. Attempted in several British estates (Park Hill, Sheffield).

---

## T

**Tectonics**
The art and science of building construction. Tectonic honesty — showing how a building is actually assembled — is a brutalist value.

**Texture**
In brutalism, texture is not applied decoration but the natural result of material and construction process. Formwork marks, aggregate patterns, and weathering are all valued as authentic surface expression.

---

## U

**Urbanism**
The study and practice of how cities are planned and experienced. Brutalism proposed radical urbanism — separating pedestrians from cars, creating raised decks, organizing services and housing in single megastructures.

---

## W

**Web Brutalism** *(Brutalist Web Design)*
A design movement in digital interface design (c. 2014–present) that applies brutalist principles to websites: minimal CSS, system fonts, visible grid, deliberate "rawness." Treats the HTML document as a structural material rather than a surface to be styled.
