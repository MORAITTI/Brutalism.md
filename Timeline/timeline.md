# **Brutalism Timeline: From Concrete to Code**

A chronological map of key events, buildings, publications, and movements in the history of brutalism — from its architectural origins to its digital revival.

---

## **Pre-History: Foundations (1920s–1940s)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1923** | Le Corbusier publishes *Vers une Architecture* — foundational text for modernism and proto-brutalism |
| **1927** | Le Corbusier's "Five Points of Architecture" formalized |
| **1928** | CIAM (Congrès Internationaux d'Architecture Moderne) founded — the institutional home of modernism |
| **1947** | Le Corbusier begins *Unité d'Habitation*, Marseille — the first fully realized béton brut building |

---

## **Birth of Brutalism (1950s)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1950** | Alison and Peter Smithson coin the term "New Brutalism" in Britain |
| **1952** | Completion of *Unité d'Habitation*, Marseille — the founding monument of brutalism |
| **1954** | Smithson's Hunstanton School, Norfolk — first British brutalist building |
| **1955** | USSR: Khrushchev's decree against "excess in construction" launches mass concrete housing |
| **1958** | First Soviet *khrushchevka* mass-production begins |

## **Golden Age (1960s)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1960** | Barbican Estate construction begins, London (completed 1982) |
| **1963** | Boston City Hall begins construction (completed 1968) — defining American brutalism |
| **1964** | Park Hill Estate, Sheffield — streets in the sky realized |
| **1966** | Reyner Banham publishes *The New Brutalism: Ethic or Aesthetic?* — the definitive critical text |
| **1967** | Habitat 67, Montreal — Moshe Safdie's modular brutalist housing for Expo 67 |
| **1968** | Trellick Tower, London begins construction (completed 1972) |

<img src="https://avatars.mds.yandex.net/i?id=383abfed56a285edcf354e8e483d9691_l-8162448-images-thumbs&n=13"/>

## **Soviet Peak (1960s–1970s)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1961** | Palace of Congresses, Moscow Kremlin — early Soviet modernism |
| **1967** | Georgian Ministry of Highways, Tbilisi — one of the USSR's most striking brutalist buildings |
| **1970** | Central Statistical Office, Kiev — Soviet administrative brutalism |
| **1974** | Druzhba Sanatorium, Yalta — crystalline Soviet brutalism |
| **1979** | Monument to the Battle of Kursk (USSR) — monumental Soviet brutalist memorial |

## **Decline and Criticism (1970s–1990s)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1972** | Pruitt-Igoe housing project, St. Louis, demolished — widely called the "death of modernism" |
| **1975** | J.G. Ballard publishes *High-Rise* — brutalist dystopian novel |
| **1977** | Charles Jencks declares "the death of modern architecture" |
| **1980s** | Postmodernism rises; brutalism widely criticized as cold and inhuman |
| **1988** | Many British brutalist estates begin demolition programs |
| **1991** | USSR dissolved; Soviet brutalist monuments enter period of neglect |

## **Reassessment and Revival (2000s–2010s)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **2003** | Frédéric Chaubin begins photographing Soviet brutalist monuments across former USSR |
| **2011** | Chaubin publishes *CCCP: Cosmic Communist Constructions Photographed* — sparks Soviet brutalism revival |
| **2012** | Brutalist Architecture Appreciation Society founded (social media) |
| **2014** | Web Brutalism emerges as a distinct digital design movement |
| **2014** | Brutalist Websites directory launched |
| **2015** | *High-Rise* film adaptation (Ben Wheatley) — brutalism in mainstream cinema |

<img src="https://gbcdn.mrgcdn.ru/uploads/geekbrains/public/ckeditor_assets/pictures/7645/retina-cb728c6055db22833acd7d606531e96d.jpg"/>

## **Digital Era (2015–Present)**

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **2016** | Brutalism enters mainstream graphic design — spreads from underground web design |
| **2017** | Museum of Brutalist Art opens discussions (various cities) |
| **2018** | London's Barbican Estate listed as Grade II* — conservation recognition for brutalism |
| **2020** | Brutalist web design peaks; featured in major design publications |
| **2022** | Brutalism moves "underground" in web design — adopted by niche/indie projects |
| **2024** | *The Brutalist* (film, dir. Brady Corbet) wins 3 Academy Awards including Best Actor |
| **2025** | Brutalism in AI-generated design — brutalist prompts become popular aesthetic category |
