# **Brutalism in Music: Industrial Noise and Concrete Sound**

Brutalism as a philosophy — the rejection of ornamentation, the embrace of raw material, and the celebration of functional form — finds a powerful echo in music. Long before architects stopped plastering over concrete, composers and producers were tearing away the decorative layers of sound.

## **1. Industrial Music: The First Brutalist Sound**

Industrial music, emerging in the late 1970s with acts like **Throbbing Gristle**, **Einstürzende Neubauten**, and **SPK**, is perhaps the most literal musical equivalent of architectural brutalism.

- **Exposed structure** — machines, drills, steel beams used as instruments.
- **Anti-ornamentation** — rejection of melody for its own sake; sound as raw material.
- **Monumental scale** — compositions designed to overwhelm and confront.

> *"We are the wreckers of civilization."* — Throbbing Gristle

**Einstürzende Neubauten** (German for *"Collapsing New Buildings"*) practically named themselves after brutalist demolition. Their early records feature pneumatic drills, metal scaffolding, and factory noise as primary instruments.

---

## **2. Noise Music and Harsh Aesthetics**

Japanese **Noise** (Japanoise) artists like **Merzbow** and **Masami Akita** apply brutalist logic to sound:

- No compromise with listener comfort.
- Pure texture and density over structure.
- The aesthetic of **béton brut** — raw, unprocessed signal.

---

## **3. Dark Ambient and Soviet Echoes**

Artists like **Coil**, **Current 93**, and Russian projects such as **Bardoseneticcube** create soundscapes that evoke the spaces inside brutalist architecture: vast, cold, resonant, and deeply human despite their inhuman scale.

Dark ambient as a genre often draws directly from the visual vocabulary of Soviet brutalism:
- Monolithic drones mirroring monolithic facades.
- Decay textures — crumbling concrete rendered in granular synthesis.
- Silence as structural element, the way empty space defines brutalist buildings.

---

## **4. Electronic Music: Techno and the Factory**

Detroit Techno — **Underground Resistance**, **Model 500** — emerged from abandoned factories, spaces that are the American equivalent of brutalist housing estates.

Berlin Techno, born in converted power stations and bunkers (*Tresor*, *Berghain*), is inseparable from brutal concrete interiors. The music mirrors its architecture:
- Grid-based, repetitive, monumental.
- Function over beauty.
- The body as material to be processed.

## **5. Film Scores and Brutalist Soundtracks**

Several film composers have written music that directly echoes brutalist spatial experience:

| Composer | Work | Brutalist Quality |
|---|---|---|
| **Wendy Carlos** | *A Clockwork Orange* OST | Sterile, mechanical precision |
| **György Ligeti** | *2001: A Space Odyssey* | Monolithic, inhuman scale |
| **Jonny Greenwood** | *There Will Be Blood* OST | Raw, aggressive, unresolved |
| **Ennio Morricone** | *The Good, the Bad and the Ugly* | Structural minimalism |

---

## **Conclusion**

Brutalism in music is not a genre — it is a posture. It insists that the material (sound, noise, signal) be shown as it is: unfinished, heavy, honest. From Throbbing Gristle's performance art to Berghain's dancefloor, the brutalist impulse runs through music wherever decoration is refused and raw power is embraced.
