# **Defenders & Critics: The Brutalism Debate**

No architectural movement has been more divisively loved and hated than brutalism. Below are the central arguments from both sides — the people who championed it, the people who condemned it, and why the debate still matters today.

---

## **The Defenders**

### **Reyner Banham** (1922–1988)
The most important champion of brutalism. His 1966 book *The New Brutalism: Ethic or Aesthetic?* gave the movement its intellectual framework. Banham argued that brutalism was not merely a visual style but a **moral position** — architecture as honest as possible about what it was made of and how it worked.

> *"Brutalism tries to be factual and honest about materials, structures, and services."*

Banham saw exposed concrete not as cold — but as genuinely democratic. No fake cladding to suggest something the building wasn't.

---

### **Alison and Peter Smithson** (1928–1993, 1923–2003)
The founders of New Brutalism. Their argument: architecture should reflect the **"as found"** — the material reality of the post-war world. Not romantic escapism, but honest engagement with industrial materials and urban life.

> *"Brutalism is an attempt to confront the chaos of our times."*

They saw their street-deck estates as social infrastructure — concrete neighborhoods that would build community as effectively as a Victorian street.

---

### **Le Corbusier** (1887–1965)
The grandfather of brutalism, though he never used the term. His argument for béton brut was essentially spiritual: raw concrete, left in its natural state, was **honest** in a way that plastered or clad surfaces could not be.

> *"Architecture is the masterly, correct and magnificent play of masses brought together in light."*

---

### **Frédéric Chaubin** (born 1959)
The French photographer who single-handedly revived interest in Soviet brutalism with his 2011 book *CCCP: Cosmic Communist Constructions Photographed*. Chaubin's argument: these buildings are extraordinary works of imagination — visionary, strange, and now endangered.

> *"These buildings were built to last 1000 years. Now they're being demolished after 30."*

---

### **Jonathan Meades** (born 1947)
British writer and broadcaster, one of the most articulate defenders of brutalism in contemporary culture. He argues that the destruction of brutalist buildings is driven by cultural cowardice — a preference for the comfortable and familiar over the challenging and powerful.

> *"Brutalism is architecture for adults. It does not condescend. It demands engagement."*

---

### **The SOS Brutalism Movement**
A global network founded to document and campaign for the preservation of brutalist buildings. Their argument: brutalism represents a unique period of architectural ambition — utopian, flawed, and irreplaceable. Once demolished, these buildings cannot be reconstructed.

Website: [sosbrutalism.org](https://www.sosbrutalism.org)

---

## **The Critics**

### **Charles Jencks** (1939–2019)
The postmodernist critic who declared the "death of modern architecture" in 1977, symbolically dated to the demolition of Pruitt-Igoe. Jencks argued that modernism, including brutalism, had failed because it **ignored ordinary human preferences** — for ornament, historical reference, and scale.

> *"Modern architecture died in St. Louis, Missouri on July 15, 1972 at 3:32 pm."*

---

### **Prince Charles / King Charles III** (born 1948)
In a famous 1984 speech to the Royal Institute of British Architects, the then-Prince Charles called a proposed extension to the National Gallery "a monstrous carbuncle on the face of a much-loved and elegant friend." He went on to become one of the most prominent critics of brutalist and modernist architecture.

> *"What have we done to our capital city?"*

His interventions had concrete effects — several modernist projects were cancelled or redesigned following his public objections.

---

### **Roger Scruton** (1944–2020)
Conservative philosopher and architectural critic. Scruton argued that brutalism produced buildings that **violated human scale and ignored local context**. He saw traditional architecture — proportional, ornamental, contextual — as more genuinely human.

> *"Brutalist buildings say: this is the truth. But the truth they are telling is one of power and indifference."*

---

### **Tom Wolfe** (1931–2018)
In *From Bauhaus to Our House* (1981), Wolfe savaged the entire modernist tradition, including brutalism, as an **elite ideology** imposed on ordinary people by architects who didn't have to live in their buildings.

> *"Every child goes to school in a building that looks like a duplicating-machine replacement-parts wholesale distribution warehouse."*

---

### **Residents and Communities**
Perhaps the most substantive criticism came from the people who lived in brutalist housing. Many British, French, and Soviet estates became:
- Difficult to maintain — concrete staining, water penetration, expensive heating.
- Socially isolated — the "streets in the sky" that were supposed to build community often failed.
- Associated with poverty — as middle-class tenants left, the estates concentrated disadvantage.

---

## **The Live Debate: Preservation vs. Demolition**

Today the debate has taken a practical form: as brutalist buildings reach the end of their planned lifespans, decisions must be made.

**For Preservation:**
- These buildings represent irreplaceable architectural history.
- Many are structurally sound and could be repurposed.
- Demolition is expensive, carbon-intensive, and permanent.
- Many have developed genuine communities and identities.

**For Demolition:**
- Some buildings have genuine structural and social problems.
- Land values in cities make redevelopment economically irresistible.
- Some residents actively want to leave.
- Retrofitting for modern energy standards can be prohibitively expensive.

---

## **Conclusion: Why the Debate Matters**

Brutalism's ongoing controversy is not really about concrete. It is about what architecture is **for**: beauty or function? Elite vision or democratic need? The permanent or the changing? 

The debate about brutalism is the debate about modernity itself — and that is why it refuses to be settled.
