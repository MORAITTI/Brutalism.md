# **Brutalism in Fashion: Wearable Concrete**

Fashion, like architecture, can be brutal. A lineage of designers has applied brutalist logic — weight, rawness, structural honesty, the rejection of ornament — to clothing and footwear. These are garments that do not flatter; they impose.

## **1. What Makes Fashion "Brutalist"?**

Brutalist fashion shares its architectural principles:

- **Structural exposure** — seams, construction lines, and joinery are visible and intentional.
- **Weight and mass** — heavy fabrics, exaggerated silhouettes, non-body-conforming shapes.
- **Material honesty** — the quality and texture of fabric is central, not hidden.
- **Anti-decoration** — no superfluous embellishment; every element serves function or structural expression.
- **Monochrome dominance** — black, white, grey, raw linen. Color is rarely the point.
- **Confrontation with the body** — clothes that do not accommodate or flatter, but challenge.

## **2. Key Designers**

### **Rei Kawakubo / Comme des Garçons**
If there is one designer who embodies brutalist fashion most completely, it is Rei Kawakubo. Since the early 1980s she has designed:

- Clothes with deliberately asymmetric, volumetric, anti-body shapes.
- Exposed unfinished edges — hems deliberately left raw.
- Deconstruction of traditional garment construction made visible.
- Black as default — the absence of color as statement.

Her 1982 Paris debut was called "Hiroshima chic" by hostile press — and thereby accidentally named the confrontational aesthetic she was pioneering.

### **Martin Margiela (Maison Margiela)**
Margiela's defining contribution: the **inside out**. He reversed garments so their construction — the seams, the basting threads, the interfacing — became the external surface. Pure tectonic honesty.

Key gestures:
- Garments made from other garments — deconstructed tailoring.
- Deliberately unfinished hems.
- White label with numbers replacing the brand name — anonymity over signature.
- Clothes photographed on headless, identity-free models.

<img src="https://i.pinimg.com/vwebp/1200x/64/7c/ae/647caef35ab4b3d1e3605575095e7102.webp"/>

### **Rick Owens**
Owens works with volume and weight to create garments that suggest architecture more than dressing:

- Massive, draped silhouettes.
- Heavy quality fabrics — leather, thick cotton, wool — used at scale.
- Palette: black, white, grey, bone. Rarely anything else.
- Footwear designed with the weight and angularity of construction materials.

<img src="https://i.pinimg.com/vwebp/1200x/08/d4/a2/08d4a2f061c9c4162fbf3d27ea5f3039.webp"/>

### **Helmut Lang**
Lang's brutalism was more restrained — minimalist, industrial:
- Transparent industrial materials (PVC, nylon) used as fabric.
- Exposed construction hardware (zippers as decoration, not fastening).
- Medical and technical references.

<img src="https://i.pinimg.com/1200x/1d/55/23/1d5523ec2323e6b26de8eee53ff48c7a.jpg"/>

### **Ann Demeulemeester**
- Long, fluid structures with structural weight.
- The layered draped form as architecture.
- Palette: black, white, ivory.

## **3. Brutalist Footwear**

Footwear has its own brutalist tradition, where the shoe becomes a concrete block:

| Designer/Brand | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Margiela** | Deconstructed heels, reversed uppers |
| **Rick Owens** | Wedge sneakers with architectural sole mass |
| **Balenciaga** (post-2015) | Triple S — mass and weight as explicit design |
| **Prada** (industrial era) | Nylon as anti-luxury material |
| **Yohji Yamamoto** | Volume displaced downward into footwear |

## **4. Brutalism in Soviet and Russian Fashion**

Soviet fashion was materially brutalist by circumstance:
- State-produced fabrics were heavy and utilitarian.
- Silhouettes were functional, not flattering.
- Military and industrial clothing aesthetics were normative.

Contemporary Russian designers like **Gosha Rubchinskiy** (now Rubchinsky+Karinsky) built an internationally recognized aesthetic from post-Soviet brutalism:
- Working-class Soviet sportswear as high fashion.
- Crumbling concrete courtyards as runway.
- Typography taken directly from Soviet packaging.

## **5. Brutalist Fashion Photography**

The settings matter as much as the clothes:

- **Nick Knight** — industrial spaces, exposed infrastructure.
- **Peter Lindbergh** — his preference for black-and-white, raw texture, and documentary rawness is quintessentially brutalist.
- **Juergen Teller** — anti-glamour, flash photography, brutal honesty.

---

## **6. Brutalism in Accessories and Jewelry**

- **Alexander Calder** — sculptures worn as jewelry; weight and abstract form.
- **Georg Jensen** (certain pieces) — raw silver with structural, anti-decorative forms.
- Concrete jewelry — a growing design category using actual cast concrete in wearable objects.

---

## **Conclusion**

Brutalist fashion is fashion that refuses to be comfortable — with itself, with the body, with conventional ideas of beauty. Like a Barbican flat or a béton brut facade, it presents its structure without apology. It is heavy, honest, and occasionally magnificent.
